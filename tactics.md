For any game there are a few basic guidelines you can follow to start thinking about a strategy:

What is my personal goal? (Max out on victory points? Eliminate the others? Achieve something before anyone else does?)

Which resources are available to me? (Do others have the same resources? Which resources are exclusively for me?)

What information is available? (What info does everyone have? What info is only known to me/my team? Do I know what my rivals know? Do I know what I don’t know?)

And finally on a very abstract level: What is the most effective/efficient way to reach my goal using the resources/information available to me.

 

Let's talk about Love Letter! There is a lot of randomness involved in Love Letter. Therefore our goal will be to max out our chances. We'll assume the worst case, which is that every other player also plays in the best way possible for himself (That's a big assumption, but if they don’t play in the best way possible, it’s even better for us.)

 

For the sake of this theory, we are playing with 4 players:

What is my personal goal? Assuming we want to win the game, our goal is to gather 3 hearts. Therefore we have to win three rounds before someone else does. We could say we have to win as much as possible, but that would be only half the truth. There can be situations where we can decide who else is going to win (or to lose). Always decide against the player with the most hearths since he (or she, I will refrain from gendering for the sake of readability) is our biggest threat. To win a single round we have to finish the deck and hold on to the highest card still in the game or to be the last player standing. Since there is a lot of luck involved we can't decide on a strategy before the game starts. We'll try to achieve both winning conditions at the same time (although we will have to decide at some point for one strategy)

Which resources are available to me? Our only resources are the two cards in our hand and a lot of information. This brings me to the biggest point in Love Letter:

What information is available?

The information everyone has -All open, already played cards in front of every player -The amount of hearths of every player -wrong guessed Guards (if still up to date) -won Barons (if still up to date) -Played Countess (just a speculation)

Information available only to us: -The two cards in our hand -played Priests (if up to date) -played Barons (if it was a draw and still up to date)

Know what we don’t know: -We know which cards are still in the game. We don’t know how they are distributed. -We now, that other players might have more information, since they played a priest etc.. We don’t know what they know.

So, what's the strategy? Depends on the situation we are currently in. Luckily there are only a few possible two-card combinations we can get. We talk about all of them.

Guard/Guard: We have to play the guard. What do we guess? It depends on the information we have. Do we know someone's card or have a strong guess? (Because of played Priests, Barons, and Countesses) We could guess their cards. But keep in mind: We want to eliminate the players with the most hearts first. They are the threat. Else: We look around and identify all the possible cards. Is there any card still 2x in the game? Guess the card with the highest value (e.g. if we go first in a new round, guess Prince). If some cards are already played, so that no card is more than once available, guess the "dangerous" cards: Our second card is a Guard, so we have to fear the Baron (we would always lose) and the king (we would have to get rid of that card or else the player using the King will guess our card next turn). If there are only a few cards left in the deck, guess the card with the highest score still in the game, since players will try to keep the valuable cards till the end. (*footnote: If we don’t have any clue whose card to guess, guess the card of the player sitting on our right (the player who goes right before us.) That way we give information about that player to all other players and they are more likely to guess his card/Baron him. Therefore we lower the chances of getting kicked out ourselves.)

Guard/Priest If we have information and try to target a specific player, we play the Guard (see Guard/Guard). In every other case we should play the Priest for a few reasons: It's safer to have a Guard then a priest, since a Guard can't be guessed. The only situation a Priest out values the Guard, is when someone would play a Baron on us, and the player using the Baron has a Guard himself (will not happen, I guarantee it.). Also, the more the game progresses, the more information we can gather, therefore the value of the Guard rises. It makes sense to hold on to the Guard. In the meantime, we can gather even more information using the Priest, making our Guard stronger. To choose a target for the Priest, see Guard/Guard. It's the same idea. Eliminate the strongest player first, thus gather information about him first.

Guard/Baron We play the Guard for obvious reasons. See Guard/Guard.

Guard/Handmaid That’s a tricky one. First, if somebody knows our card and there is a reasonable chance that they could have or draw a Guard themselves we should play the Maid for that reason (In the future I will call this Reason One). Also, if many of the "twin" cards were played but not the Maid, there is a high chance people will start to guess Maid. Second, the longer we wait, the better the Guard gets, since we can gather more information. In a round with 4 players, we get to see 4 (out of 16) more cards, thus our guess will have a much higher success rate. Reasons not to play the Maid would be that the Maid is slightly more valuable than the average card value of 3,3 (and a median of 3). So we want to hold on to the card to have a higher chance in the compare-scenario. Also, the Maid is a great card to protect our other card. We can hope on drawing some really high value and play the Maid to survive one more turn. The Guard/Maid combination is one of the few unclear situations we can get. In the most cases we can solve the question what to play, if we think about the given information (Part 3).

Guard/Prince There are three situations in which we should play the Prince. First, Reason One, or second we don't know what to guess and there is a good chance that the high value cards are still in the game. In that case we could discard our own card to draw a better one. Or finally, if we know who got the princess (best case there is only one enemy left) we can force them to discard the card and loose the round. (Don't forget: your biggest threat is the player with the most hearts). In any other case we play the Guard. See Guard/Guard.

Guard/King If we would play the king, we would get somebody's card, and they would get a Guard. In their turn they most likely guess our new card and kick us out. That's a bad idea. There are only two constellations, in which we should play the king: First, Reason One, or second there are not enough cards left so the game ends before the changing player gets another turn and we are very sure he has a higher card than a king. In any other situation we should play the Guard. See Guard/Guard.

Guard/Countess So we have the second best card in the game and a Guard. I see two reasons we could play the Countess: First, Reason One. Or second we want to hold on to the Guard, because we can guarantee to guess the last players card somehow. In any other situation we should play the Guard. See Guard/Guard.

Guard/Princess We play the Guard for obvious reasons. See Guard/Guard.

Priest/Priest We play the Priest. See Guard/Priest

Priest/Baron We play the Baron for Reason One. Else we play the Priest. See Guard/Priest.

Priest/Handmaid We play the Handmaid for Reason One. Else we play the Priest. See Guard/Priest.

Priest/Prince We play the Prince for Reason One, if we want to knock out a player with a princess or if we think it makes sense to discard the Priest. Else we play the Priest. See Guard/Priest.

Priest/King We play the King for Reason One or if we know who got a Countess or the Princess and the round is nearly over. Else we play the Priest. See Guard/Priest.

Priest/Countess We play the Countess for Reason One. Else we play the Priest. See Guard/Priest.

Priest/Princess We play the Priest. See Guard/Priest.

Baron/Baron We play the Baron. But who do we challenge? If we have no information available, we should challenge the player with the most hearts. If we win, we kicked out our biggest threat, if we lose; we give at least some information to the other players, so they can kick him out. If you know someone's card for a reason and it's cheaper than yours, challenge him. If you go first and there is no information at all, challenge the player on your right side, for reasons see Guard/Guard. In general, we try to challenge the player with the cheapest card, so we give as little information away as possible.

Baron/Handmaid We play the Handmaid for Reason One. Else, we play the Baron. Since the average is 3,3 (and a median of 3) we can play the Baron and should win more than 50% of the times. For choosing a target see Baron/Baron. In some cases it is viable to play the Handmaid, just to protect the baron, because we know, that the card we will draw is very high, or to protect ourselves from getting kicked out. It's a very situational choice.

Baron/Prince We play the Prince for Reason One or if we want to knock out a player with a princess or if we think it makes sense to discard the Baron. Else we play the Baron. See Baron/Baron.

Baron/King We play the King for Reason One or if we know who got the princess and consider it safe to trade her. Else we play the Baron. See Baron/Baron.

Baron/Countess We play the Countess for Reason One. Else we play the Baron. See Baron/Baron.

Baron/Princess We play the Baron. See Baron/Baron.

Handmaid/Handmaid We play the Handmaid.

Handmaid/Prince We play the Prince if we want to knock out a player with a princess. Else we play the Handmaid. Reason One doesn’t apply, because we are shielded by the Maid.

Handmaid/King We play the King if we know who got the princess and consider it safe to trade her. Else we play the Handmaid.

Handmaid/Countess We play the Handmaid.

Handmaid/Princess We play the Handmaid. And we put a face on, as if we have to make a hard decision.

Prince/Prince We play the Prince and we if we know who got the princess, we choose that person. Else, we try to discard a high value card. So, what information is available? If Reason One is applied: we just played the card that they knew, so they are not going to guess the exact same card.

Prince/King We play the King for Reason One, or if we know who got the Countess and consider it safe to trade her. We don't want to trade into the princess, because the Prince can discard her. Else we play the Prince. See Prince/Prince.

Prince/Countess We are forced to play the Countess.

Prince/Princess We play the Prince. See Prince/Prince.

King/Countess We are forced to play the Countess.

King/Princess We play the King and we try to change our Princess for a Guard, so we can kick out the player with the princess. If no information is available and the round is about to end, we give the Princess to the weakest player. If the round just started we give the princess to the player with the most hearts, since it is pretty likely for us to draw a Guard or a Prince. (Perhaps some other player will get the hint and kick him out.)

 

tl;dr: Love Letter

Look at all the open cards and think of the cards, which are still in the game. Make decisions based on that information. Always play against the winning player. If in doubt play the card with the lowest value. Else:

Detailed tactic map:

- Guard/Guard uses known hand facts first, then the most likely remaining card values, then the player on your right if nothing is known.
- Guard/Priest usually keeps the Guard and uses Priest only when you do not already have a strong target or a strong reason to guess now.
- Guard/Handmaid uses Handmaid when the table can reasonably point a Guard at you, or when you want to protect a strong follow-up card.
- Guard/Prince uses Prince when you can hit a known Princess, when your hand is weak, or when you want to cycle into a better play next turn.
- Guard/King uses King only when the swap is likely to improve your hand or when the round is so close that the return swap is unlikely to punish you.
- Guard/Countess uses Countess only when it is forced or when holding Guard for a better guess matters more than the Countess tempo loss.
- Priest/Priest is a pure information line. If both cards are Priest, the bot should just take the info and keep future options open.
- Priest/Baron uses Baron when the target is a real compare candidate; otherwise Priest is usually better because it keeps more tempo and more hidden information.
- Priest/Handmaid uses Handmaid when you need protection or when your other card is more valuable for later comparison.
- Priest/Prince uses Prince when you know a player is likely sitting on Princess or another dead-end card, otherwise Priest is the safer line.
- Priest/King uses King when the target is known to be strong or when a late-round swap can secure the round.
- Priest/Countess uses Countess only when it is forced or when you are protecting a high-value turn later.
- Baron/Baron usually challenges the strongest threat when you have no better read, but can switch to the weakest target when you already know someone is cheap enough to beat.
- Baron/Handmaid uses Handmaid only when survival or protecting the Baron line matters more than taking the compare now.
- Baron/Prince uses Prince when the target looks like Princess, otherwise Baron is the compare card that usually converts better.
- Baron/King uses King when you know the trade is safe and probably improves the compare outcome.
- Handmaid/Prince uses Prince if you want to flush a known Princess or force a bad hand out of a player, otherwise Handmaid.
- Handmaid/King uses King only when you know the trade will not hand the round to a threat.
- Handmaid/Princess almost always means Handmaid because preserving your own life is worth more than the direct play.
- Prince/Prince should usually target the known Princess player first, then the player most likely to be holding a high-value card.
- Prince/King is mostly a late-game or information-rich line: trade only when the known hand on the other side is worth the risk.
- King/Princess is a deliberate hand-shift line: give the Princess away only when the target is weak or when the round is close enough that you can convert it back.
- If you have no strong read, aim guess-based cards at the player on your right so the table leaks more information onto them.

Hard mode note:

- The hard bot now uses the same tactics, but it values exact known reads, information cards, and follow-up safety more aggressively.
- In practice that means it guesses more confidently when a Priest read is current, prefers stronger compare targets more often, and is less random in borderline turns.

More tactics worth adding:

- Track whether a Priest reveal is still current. If the same player later discards that exact card, stop treating the old reveal as live.
- Prefer information on the strongest threat, but switch to the weakest target when the card is a compare card and you want the best win chance.
- Keep Guard guesses tied to what is still possible in the deck, not just what feels strong.
- Use Prince on yourself when your hand is bad or when you want to cycle into a better play next turn.
- Use Handmaid when you want to protect a strong follow-up card or when the table is likely to point a Guard at you.
- Use King only when the trade is likely to help more than hurt, or when the round is so close that the swap probably will not come back to punish you.
- Use Baron and Dowager Queen when you have a real read on the target or when your own remaining card strength makes the compare favorable.
- Treat Countess as forced tempo loss unless it is protecting you from a worse line.
- Track discard piles, because a large discard pile can mean a player is either dangerous or already partially solved.
- When you have no strong read, prefer the player on your right for guess-based cards so the table leaks information onto that player.

Is the bot smart enough?

Right now: mostly yes for basic heuristics, not fully for deeper strategy.

It already knows about remembered hand facts, current discard history, forced Countess plays, card values, target legality, and it scores plays with those signals. That is enough for a solid rule-based bot.

What it does not do yet is full game-tree search, long-horizon opponent modeling, or endgame calculation beyond simple heuristics. So it is smart enough to play sensibly, but not smart enough to always find the best line in messy endgames.

Premium-only cards not covered enough above:

These cards are officially used in 5 to 8 player Love Letter Premium games. The rulebook gives the card intent and constraints; the strategy guidance below turns that into practical heuristics.

- Assassin
  - Tactical role: hidden anti-Guard trap.
  - Best use: do not "use" it actively; its value is in staying hidden in hand until a Guard targets you.
  - If a Guard-heavy board is still live, holding Assassin is often safer than it looks.
  - Once revealed, its deterrent value drops because the table will stop trying to solve you with Guard and will switch to Prince, compare cards, or forced targeting.
  - Bot heuristic: value Assassin more highly early or mid-round when Guards are still unaccounted for.

- Jester
  - Tactical role: side-bet on the round winner.
  - Best use: target the player most likely to win the current round, not necessarily the match leader.
  - Strongest late in the round when the likely winner is clearer.
  - Weakest early when too many unknown cards remain.
  - If two players look close, prefer the one with the stronger visible hand path, stronger discard pile, or better protection state.
  - Bot heuristic: scale Jester up as the round narrows and use threat plus board position to pick the target.

- Cardinal
  - Tactical role: disruption plus selective information.
  - Best use: swap a strong-looking hand away from the current threat, or upgrade your own weak hand by including yourself in the swap.
  - If you include yourself, the peek becomes much more valuable because you can confirm what you ended up with or what you handed away.
  - Late in the round, Cardinal can swing the whole round by moving a Princess, Countess, or other high-value card off the likely winner.
  - It gets much worse when Sycophant or Handmaid makes the legal target set awkward.
  - Bot heuristic: prefer Cardinal lines that include self and one strong opponent, especially if that opponent is the match leader.

- Baroness
  - Tactical role: information compression.
  - Best use: look at two hands when the table is still unsolved, especially before a future Guard, Baron, Prince, or Dowager Queen line.
  - If one clear threat exists, look at that player first; otherwise use it to solve two uncertain players instead of over-checking one already-known target.
  - Baroness is much stronger when your follow-up turns can convert the info into elimination or protection.
  - Bot heuristic: prioritize Baroness earlier than pure tempo cards when hand knowledge is low and several players remain active.

- Sycophant
  - Tactical role: force the next targeted play to include a chosen player.
  - Best use: point it at the current leader so the next action has to pressure them.
  - Advanced use: point it at yourself only when being chosen helps you, such as setting up Handmaid protection, constraining a harmless card, or baiting a Guard into an Assassin.
  - It is mainly a manipulation card, so its value depends heavily on turn order and what the next player is likely to hold.
  - Bot heuristic: use Sycophant offensively on threats by default, with rare self-target exceptions when the self-line is clearly safer or more profitable.

- Count
  - Tactical role: end-of-round tiebreak and showdown booster.
  - Best use: keep Count in mind when the round looks likely to end by deck exhaustion rather than elimination.
  - Count is worth more when you already expect to survive and less when the table is still explosive.
  - Two Counts stack, so discard history matters a lot in premium endgames.
  - Bot heuristic: increase Count value as deck count shrinks and as active-player count falls.

- Constable
  - Tactical role: elimination insurance that can convert a loss into a token.
  - Best use: accept risky lines more willingly when Constable is already in your discard pile.
  - Constable becomes especially dangerous near the match-winning token threshold because a player can lose the round but still gain the deciding token.
  - If an opponent has shown or is likely to have Constable in discard, be careful about "eliminating" them into a point they actually want.
  - Bot heuristic: once Constable is in your discard pile, survival matters a little less and token-race tactics matter more.

- Dowager Queen
  - Tactical role: reverse Baron.
  - Best use: challenge a player when your remaining card is low and theirs is likely high.
  - It is strongest with real information from Priest, Baroness, Cardinal peek, or prior compare effects.
  - It is also strong as a leader-punish tool because high-value hands are more likely to be held by players trying to close the round.
  - Bot heuristic: use Dowager Queen only when the target is known or strongly suspected to be above your kept card; otherwise treat it as a risky situational compare.

- Bishop
  - Tactical role: token-race guess, not pure removal.
  - Best use: fire it when you have a strong read and gaining a token matters more than preserving the target in a bad state.
  - Because the target redraws after a correct hit, Bishop gives less direct board control than Guard and should be treated more like a scoring burst.
  - Bishop becomes excellent when one token would immediately win the match or create a decisive race advantage.
  - In end-of-round comparisons, remember the Princess still beats Bishop despite Bishop's printed 9.
  - Bot heuristic: prefer Bishop when the match race matters more than the local round state, and when the read is precise rather than merely probabilistic.

How these should influence bot priorities:

- Premium information cards (`Baroness`, `Cardinal`) should be stronger when many players remain and knowledge is low.
- Premium scoring cards (`Bishop`, `Jester`, `Constable`) should care more about token race than simple round survival.
- Premium manipulation cards (`Sycophant`, `Cardinal`) should lean harder into targeting the match leader.
- Premium compare cards (`Dowager Queen`) should require stronger evidence than ordinary tempo cards.
- `Assassin` and `Count` are mostly state-dependent value cards: one deters Guards, the other matters when the deck is close to empty.
